## Overview

The LCT 240 PRO is the entry-level XLR cardioid condenser microphone in the LCT recording series from Lewitt, the Vienna-based maker founded in 2010 by former AKG product manager Roman Perschon [1]. It is a simplified derivative of the original LCT 240, with the pad, low-cut filter, and clipping-history indicator removed to lower cost. Marketed for home recording, vocals, podcasting, and streaming, it requires an interface or preamp supplying 48V phantom power.

## Scientific Validity

$$ \Large \text{0.6} $$

Manufacturer specifications [1] list an equivalent noise level (self-noise) of 19 dB(A), a maximum SPL of 142 dBSPL at 0.5% THD, and a frequency range of 20–20,000 Hz; the S/N ratio derives to approximately 75 dB(A). The maximum SPL of 142 dBSPL provides excellent headroom, while self-noise of 19 dB(A) is acceptable but sits close to a problematic margin, and the ~75 dB(A) S/N ratio is moderate. An interactive frequency-response plot [2] shows a broadly even response with a presence peak of roughly +5 dB around 8–10 kHz and low-end roll-off below about 100 Hz — an intentional coloration rather than flat reproduction. No independent numeric distortion data is published. Overall measured performance is moderate.

## Technology Level

$$ \Large \text{0.3} $$

The LCT 240 PRO is built from mature, industry-standard components: a 17mm back-electret capsule, a transformerless FET output stage with surface-mount construction, a fixed cardioid pattern, and a gold-plated XLR connector [1]. Lewitt engineers its microphones in-house rather than rebadging OEM hardware, which is a genuine positive. However, these technologies are now ubiquitous and easily replicated, the model carries no proprietary patents (Lewitt's patented clipping-history and dual-output features appear only on higher-tier models), they offer no lasting competitive advantage, and the signal chain is purely analog with no digital integration. The result is a below-average technology level for a deliberately simplified entry microphone.

## Cost-Performance

$$ \Large \text{0.5} $$

The current market price of the LCT240pro is 149 USD [1]. The cheapest equivalent-or-better option identified is the Behringer B-1 at 69 USD [3], giving CP = 69 USD / 149 USD = 0.4631. The B-1 provides XLR balanced output, 48V phantom power, a fixed cardioid pattern, 20–20,000 Hz coverage, a switchable low-cut filter, and a -10 dB pad, and its key microphone metrics are equivalent-or-better [4][5]:

- Equivalent noise (self-noise): 13 dB(A) vs 19 dB(A) (better)
- S/N ratio: 81 dB(A) vs ~75 dB(A) (better)
- Maximum SPL: 138/148 dBSPL with 0/-10 dB pad vs 142 dBSPL at a 0.5% THD reference (condition-dependent equivalent-or-better)
- Frequency response: 20–20,000 Hz vs 20–20,000 Hz (equivalent)

The comparison is provisional because both products are documented by manufacturer specifications and the B-1 maximum-SPL condition is not published on the same THD basis as the LCT240pro.

## Reliability & Support

$$ \Large \text{0.7} $$

Lewitt provides a 2-year limited warranty covering material and manufacturing faults [6]. Construction is simple and robust: an all-metal zinc die-cast body and a passive analog signal path with no digital circuitry and no moving parts beyond the diaphragm. Support is manufacturer-direct, backed by a distributor network spanning 70+ countries and a dedicated US branch opened in 2022. No product recalls, service bulletins, or failure-rate data exist, so failure rate is treated as unknown. Firmware is not applicable to this passive analog microphone. The reliability and support profile is above average.

## Rationality of Design Philosophy

$$ \Large \text{0.6} $$

The LCT 240 PRO follows a rational, cost-focused philosophy: modern surface-mount transformerless circuitry, automated per-unit testing, mass production, and a practical zinc die-cast body, with costs directed toward functional elements rather than luxury materials or brand premium. Its "record-ready" voicing is an intentional non-flat tuning with a presence peak around 8–10 kHz rather than fidelity-first flat reproduction, so it does not qualify as a fully measurement-driven design, though the coloration is a legitimate, audible engineering choice. The cost reduction relative to the parent LCT 240 was achieved by removing genuinely useful functions (pad and high-pass filter), so it represents an entry-tier simplification rather than a performance advance. The philosophy is generally sound.

## Advice

The LCT240pro is a straightforward XLR cardioid condenser for home recording, podcasting, and streaming, and needs an interface or preamp supplying 48V phantom power. Its strengths are high maximum SPL and a durable metal body; self-noise and S/N ratio are only moderate, and the presence peak around 8–10 kHz means some sources will need EQ rather than truly minimal post-processing. Buyers who want lower noise, a built-in pad, or a low-cut filter can obtain equal-or-better measured performance for less outlay, so weigh the added switching features of cheaper alternatives before purchasing.
