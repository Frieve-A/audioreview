## Overview

The BQEYZ Wind is a wired in-ear monitor in the brand's Weather Series, released around 2024 and currently selling at 239 USD [1]. It is a fully passive, analog IEM pairing a 10mm LCP composite-diaphragm dynamic driver with a self-developed bass/midrange bone-conduction driver, housed in a CNC-machined aluminum shell with a detachable 0.78mm 2-pin cable available in 2.5mm, 3.5mm, and 4.4mm terminations [1]. BQEYZ is an established Chinese IEM maker that has iterated bone-conduction transducers across multiple Weather Series generations, and markets the Wind toward vocal and ACG content.

## Scientific Validity

$$ \Large \text{0.7} $$

Third-party frequency-response measurement shows a deviation from the Harman IEM target of STD 1.87 dB [2], indicating close adherence to the research-based target curve and good tonal balance. Frequency response is the dominant applicable metric for a passive IEM, and on this primary axis the Wind sits at the borderline of transparent tonal performance. Manufacturer specifications list a frequency response of 20 Hz–20 kHz, an impedance of 16 Ω, and a sensitivity of 105 dB/mW [1]; no manufacturer THD or S/N figures are published, and no third-party distortion measurement was located. Based on the available measured frequency-response data, overall tonal performance is good.

## Technology Level

$$ \Large \text{0.6} $$

The Wind is a genuine in-house design rather than a rebadged OEM/ODM product, and BQEYZ has self-developed its own bass/midrange bone-conduction driver (copper shell, high-toughness steel reed, low-temperature laser welding) across several Weather Series generations, reflecting real accumulated transducer know-how. These factors lift the score above baseline. However, the constituent technologies are otherwise common and established as of 2026: the 10mm LCP composite-diaphragm dynamic driver, the CNC-machined aluminum shell, and the bone-conduction hybrid concept are all widely used and already appeared in BQEYZ's own earlier models, making this iteration within an established design space rather than a category-first. No patents were located, and as a fully passive analog transducer it incorporates no digital, DSP, or circuit integration. The result is modestly above average.

## Reliability & Support

$$ \Large \text{0.5} $$

The IEM carries a 1-year warranty, with 3 months on the cable [1], which is short and weighs negatively. This is offset by a simple, robust construction: a fully passive transducer with no active electronics, housed in a CNC-machined all-metal aluminum shell with an anodized, corrosion-resistant finish, where the primary wear point — the cable — is user-replaceable via the universal 0.78mm 2-pin standard. Support is available globally but is administered primarily through authorized distributors via RMA rather than a dedicated manufacturer service network, so it is treated as average. No statistical failure-rate or longevity data exists, and no systematic manufacturing defect, recall, or service bulletin is documented for this model. The short warranty and the robust simple design offset each other, leaving the score at the baseline.

## Rationality of Design Philosophy

$$ \Large \text{0.5} $$

The Wind's measured outcome tracks the research-based Harman IEM target closely (FR deviation STD 1.87 dB) [2], a scientifically reasonable direction, and it relies on no nostalgia-driven or non-scientific approaches. Its primary low-frequency claim — tactile bass via a bone-conduction driver — rests on a real physical vibration mechanism rather than an inaudible element, so it does not warrant a deduction for non-audible claims. However, the design direction is communicated and voiced in subjective, experiential terms rather than being explicitly measurement-led, so it does not earn the measurement-focused bonus. There is no documented generational performance progression and no demonstrated audible advantage from the self-developed driver, while cost is split across performance, materials, and aesthetics without a clearly non-functional majority. No single factor decisively shifts the score, leaving a neutral philosophy at the baseline.

## Advice

The Wind delivers a well-balanced frequency response that closely tracks the Harman IEM target, and its all-metal build with a user-replaceable cable is durable and serviceable. Its weakness is value: equivalent-or-better measured IEMs such as the Truthear GATE are available for around 20 USD, so at 239 USD the Wind costs roughly twelve times the price of a comparable-performing alternative. Buyers prioritizing measured tonal accuracy per dollar should consider those far cheaper options. The Wind makes sense only for those who specifically want its particular tactile-bass presentation and metal construction and accept the steep price premium that entails.
