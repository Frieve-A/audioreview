## Overview

Announced at NAMM 2020 and shipping from approximately March 2020, the Pro Tools | MTRX Studio is a 1U fixed-configuration audio interface and monitor controller co-developed by Avid Technology and Digital Audio Denmark (DAD), the converter and interface specialist behind the AX32 and the original MTRX [1][3]. It is positioned between Avid's Carbon interface and the flagship modular MTRX / MTRX II line, targeting professional Pro Tools rooms, post-production facilities, broadcast studios, and Dolby Atmos monitoring rigs up to 9.1.6 channels. Current US street price is USD 4,999 [4].

## Scientific Validity

$$ \Large \text{0.8} $$

Manufacturer specifications place the analog audio path well into transparent territory. Avid publishes line/monitor output dynamic range greater than 118 dB (A-weighted) with THD+N of −110 dB at −3 dBFS (approximately 0.0003%), and mic/instrument input dynamic range greater than 120 dB (A-weighted) with THD+N of −108 dB at −3 dBFS (approximately 0.0004%) [2]. Crosstalk is specified at less than −120 dB across the analog path [2]. Sample-rate support spans 44.1 to 192 kHz at 16/24-bit [1][2]. No independent third-party bench measurements were located, so a conservative adjustment has been applied to reflect that these figures are not externally verified, but the manufacturer-published numbers themselves indicate excellent measured performance across distortion, dynamic range, and channel separation.

## Technology Level

$$ \Large \text{0.7} $$

The MTRX Studio is explicitly a partnership product: DAD contributes the 18-channel AD/DA conversion, the SPQ speaker-processing DSP (256 IIR filters distributed across 16 channels with up to 150 ms per-channel delay), and the DADman 512×512 routing matrix software, while Avid contributes the DigiLink Mini bus (64 channels to Pro Tools HDX/HD Native), EUCON control integration, and Pro|Mon 3 monitor profiles [1][3]. DigiLink is a proprietary Avid interconnect with no third-party adapter, which constitutes a durable competitive advantage inside the HDX ecosystem. SPQ and DADman represent non-trivial accumulated DSP/software expertise. However, the product was released in 2020 and by 2026 the underlying delta-sigma conversion performance is matched by far cheaper mass-market interfaces; Dante and ADAT are licensed/standard protocols; DSP-based monitor correction is now widely available from Trinnov, Genelec GLM, and Dirac; no specific patents or AI/cloud-level integration are documented; and the design is not purely in-house Avid [3].

## Cost-Performance

$$ \Large \text{1.0} $$

Review target current US market price: **USD 4,999** [4].

Comprehensive search across professional 16×16-class audio interfaces identified no product priced below USD 4,999 that simultaneously provides Pro Tools HDX/DigiLink (64 channels), built-in 64×64 Dante, 16 + 16 analog line I/O, 2 mic preamps plus 2 instrument inputs, an integrated stereo-through-9.1.6 immersive monitor controller, and a 16-channel speaker-correction DSP equivalent to SPQ (256 filters, 150 ms delay).

Function-equivalent options are more expensive: Antelope Galaxy 64 Synergy Core at USD 9,995 [5] and Apogee Symphony I/O Mk II 16×16 SE Dante + Pro Tools HD at approximately USD 9,000+ both cost roughly double. Cheaper candidates fail function equivalence: Universal Audio Apollo x16D Essentials+ (USD 2,999) lacks HDX/DigiLink, mic preamps, instrument inputs, ADAT, and an SPQ-equivalent 16-channel speaker-correction DSP; RME Fireface UFX III (USD 3,199) lacks HDX, native Dante, and a 9.1.6 monitor controller; Focusrite RedNet A16R MkII (USD 4,399.99) lacks HDX, mic preamps, monitor controller, SPQ, and headphone outputs, and does not publish THD+N. HDX cannot be added by accessory bundle because no third-party DigiLink adapter exists, so the function gap cannot be normalized within the review-target price.

Result: **CP = 1.0 (no cheaper equivalent-or-better product exists)**. The conclusion is provisional because neither the review target nor the candidates have published third-party bench measurements; comparison relies on manufacturer specifications.

## Reliability & Support

$$ \Large \text{0.7} $$

Avid provides direct manufacturer support globally with authorized repair centers, ongoing firmware delivered via the DADman control application, and an optional Extended Hardware Support plan offering zero-downtime advanced replacement and overnight shipping [6][7]. The standard hardware warranty is 1 year, which is short for a professional rackmount audio interface [6]. Standard warranty repair turnaround is approximately 4–6 weeks. Mainboard firmware has been updated (current reported version 1.1.5.1) and DADman software receives regular releases. Individual forum reports of headphone-jack failure and connection issues exist but are not backed by any aggregate failure-rate (RMA/MTBF) data published by Avid, so they are not used for scoring. The 1U metal-chassis construction and reliance on precision converters, clocking, and DSP put the design at average complexity for the category.

## Rationality of Design Philosophy

$$ \Large \text{0.8} $$

The MTRX Studio reflects a measurement- and function-focused design philosophy. Avid/DAD adopted modern delta-sigma conversion, DSP-based speaker correction (SPQ: 256 filters, 150 ms delay), and software-controlled multi-protocol routing [1][3]. Cost allocation is directed toward concrete user-facing functionality — precision converters, SPQ DSP, dual mic preamps, Dante/DigiLink/ADAT connectivity, and integrated immersive monitor control — rather than aesthetics or non-audible elements. Proprietary technologies are each tied to demonstrable function (SPQ for immersive monitor calibration, DADman for routing flexibility, DigiLink for native Pro Tools HDX integration). No occult or inaudible-effect claims appear in product communication. The product is a sibling of the full MTRX rather than a successor exceeding it in published specs, and integration stops at appropriate digital control without AI/cloud advancement, so it does not earn additional credit for model progression or innovation, but the existence justification as dedicated audio hardware via the Pro Tools HDX ecosystem is clear.

## Advice

The MTRX Studio is recommended primarily for facilities that need Pro Tools HDX or HD Native integration, built-in 64-channel Dante, and an integrated Dolby Atmos monitor controller in a single 1U chassis — the specific combination at USD 4,999 has no cheaper equivalent on the market. Manufacturer-published audio performance specifications are excellent on paper, but independent third-party measurements are not available, so prospective buyers who rely on bench-verified performance should treat the conversion specs as provisional. If your workflow does not require DigiLink, the same audio quality and simpler immersive monitoring can be obtained more cheaply with combinations such as a Universal Audio Apollo x16D plus an external monitor controller, or with a PC + general-purpose Dante interface; the MTRX Studio's value proposition is the integrated HDX + Dante + SPQ + 9.1.6 monitor controller bundle, not raw conversion performance. The 1-year warranty is short for the price, so the optional Extended Hardware Support plan is worth considering for production-critical deployments.
