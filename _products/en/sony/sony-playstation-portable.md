## Overview

The Sony PlayStation Portable (PSP), launched in 2004, was officially marketed by Sony as the "21st century Walkman" [6]. Beyond its primary function as a handheld gaming console, Sony positioned it as a comprehensive multimedia entertainment system with significant audio capabilities. The device featured support for multiple audio codecs including MP3, ATRAC3plus, PCM, WMA, and AAC, built-in stereo speakers, and dedicated audio processing through a Wolfson WM8973G DAC. While ambitious in scope as a convergence device, the PSP's audio implementation reflected compromises inherent in its gaming-first design philosophy.

## Technology Level

$$ \Large \text{0.5} $$

The PSP employed appropriate technology for its 2004-2005 era, featuring a dedicated Media Engine for multimedia processing alongside its main Allegrex processor [3]. The Wolfson WM8973G provided stereo output with built-in equalizer functionality. Sony leveraged its Walkman heritage but the implementation focused on multimedia convergence rather than cutting-edge audio innovation. The technology represented standard contemporary solutions without significant advancement beyond general-purpose integrated approaches.

## Cost-Performance

The PSP-1000 currently sells for approximately 90 USD in the used market [4]. ## Reliability & Support

$$ \Large \text{0.7} $$

The PSP featured robust construction with simple audio circuitry that was inherently resistant to degradation. While early PSP 1000 models experienced some speaker crackling issues and headphone jack detection problems, these were primarily software-related and addressed in later firmware updates [5]. Sony provided comprehensive global support infrastructure with adequate warranty repair service throughout the product lifecycle. The basic analog audio output path and solid-state construction minimized potential failure points compared to more complex contemporary devices. Parts availability and third-party repair options remain viable for most common issues.

## Rationality of Design Philosophy

$$ \Large \text{0.3} $$

Sony's "21st century Walkman" marketing created audio expectations that the gaming-first implementation failed to meet [6]. The design philosophy demonstrated several rationality limitations: proprietary ATRAC format emphasis without measurable audio benefits over established standards, gaming-first priority resulted in audio implementation compromises despite marketing claims, higher costs compared to equivalent multimedia functionality devices without corresponding performance benefits. While the convergence concept showed innovation, the execution failed to deliver transparent-level audio performance and lacked cost-effectiveness for multimedia applications. The approach represents conservative multimedia integration rather than advancing audio technology or achieving cost-performance optimization.

## Advice

The PSP represents an interesting historical convergence device that attempted to bridge gaming and audio functionality before the smartphone era. For collectors or those seeking a retro gaming experience, the current used market prices around 90 USD (PSP-1000) offer reasonable value among gaming handhelds with multimedia capabilities. However, independent system-level measurement data is unavailable, preventing evaluation of the PSP's actual audio performance. Those interested in serious multimedia applications should consider alternatives like the Microsoft Zune 30GB or dedicated audio players from the same era. The PSP is best viewed as a gaming console with basic audio functionality rather than a legitimate portable audio solution.
