## Overview

TOMOCA Electronics Co., Ltd. (トモカ電気株式会社), founded October 1970 in Akihabara, Tokyo, operates two product brands [2]. TOMOCA Products covers professional broadcast field accessories including VU meters, portable audio monitors, earphone amplifiers, audio faders, and intercoms; the TOMOCA brand covers microphones, passive speakers, attenuators, and cables [1]. A dedicated Technical Division established in 1983 manufactures broadcast equipment for professional clients including NHK, TBS, NTV, Fuji-TV, and TV Asahi. The company employs 45 people and its Akihabara PRO SHOP is a recognized specialist destination for broadcast connectors and cable assemblies in Japan [2].

## Scientific Validity

$$ \Large \text{0.4} $$

All evaluation relies on manufacturer-published specifications; no independent third-party measurements exist for any product in either TOMOCA brand [1][2].

The EM-9600 shotgun microphone presents the most concerning profile. Its manufacturer-stated S/N ratio is 40 dB — significantly insufficient for professional microphone applications — and its FR spans only 60–16,000 Hz, which is notably narrower than full audible range coverage. MaxSPL is stated at 120 dB. Multiple specifications fall short of acceptable levels for this model.

The NPM-727 boundary microphone covers FR 20–20,000 Hz, representing full audible range. However, manufacturer-stated MaxSPL of 110 dB SPL at 1% THD is below the level considered adequate for broadcast microphone applications.

The PMA-4B 4-channel audio monitor specifies FR as 80–15,000 Hz. The 15 kHz upper limit is narrower than expected for a full-bandwidth professional monitoring device.

The EA-246 earphone amplifier publishes no audio-quality-relevant specifications — no FR, THD, S/N ratio, or dynamic range values — making measured performance assessment impossible from available data.

The TM-16 passive speaker lists FR of 70–18,000 Hz without deviation tolerances. FR is the primary evaluation metric for a passive speaker; the stated range covers a useful band but cannot be further assessed without deviation data.

All values are manufacturer specifications without independent verification, to which a conservative adjustment applies. The combination of the EM-9600's well-below-adequate S/N ratio, the NPM-727's insufficient MaxSPL, the PMA-4B's narrow FR bandwidth, and the EA-246's complete absence of audio-quality data yields a weighted company average of approximately 0.4.

## Technology Level

$$ \Large \text{0.3} $$

TOMOCA's Technical Division has produced in-house-designed broadcast accessories since 1983 [2], earning an in-house design credit. The product line is otherwise built entirely on well-established, mature technologies: balanced XLR signal routing, passive resistive attenuators and fader circuits, standard MIDI over XLR cable (as used in the TMF-QUAD remote fader system), analog VU meter movements, and LED backlighting for VU readability [1]. No patents have been identified in public records; claims of "proprietary LED illumination" describe a product-development-level customization, not a patented invention. All core technologies were codified decades ago and present no meaningful competitive barrier. The entire portfolio is analog and mechanical with no DSP, digital audio networking, software control, cloud integration, or AI functionality — the most recent protocol in the lineup (MIDI) dates to 1983. These factors drive the score substantially below the base level.

## Reliability & Support

$$ \Large \text{0.7} $$

TOMOCA has supplied professional broadcast equipment to Japan's major networks continuously for over 40 years [2]. No product recalls, systemic failures, or documented quality defects have been identified across the product line. The portfolio is dominated by simple analog designs — passive attenuators, passive faders, and basic analog amplifier circuits — whose structural simplicity confers inherent robustness and low failure probability. Formal warranty terms are not publicly documented on the official website or online shop. Support is Japan-centric, with inquiry routing through divisional contacts and repair acceptance at the Akihabara Pro Shop; no global service infrastructure is documented. No firmware is present across the entire product line.

## Rationality of Design Philosophy

$$ \Large \text{0.6} $$

TOMOCA Products and the TOMOCA brand are functional and practical in orientation: products are engineering tools for professional broadcast infrastructure, not consumer audiophile products [1][2]. No pseudo-scientific claims, occult accessories, or unsubstantiated audibility claims appear in any product documentation. Costs are directed toward engineering function — XLR connectors, balanced circuits, phantom power compatibility, field-rugged construction, and metering accuracy — all directly necessary for professional broadcast applications. The AMU-2SII exemplifies rational product evolution by miniaturizing the predecessor AMU-2IV into a smaller, more portable, lower-cost unit while maintaining equivalent metering performance.

The most significant concern is a thoroughly conservative technology stance: in 2026, the entire portfolio is analog-only with no DSP, digital audio networking (Dante, AES67, MADI), software integration, or app control. Digital audio networking and software-defined metering are now mainstream in professional broadcast environments, and the complete absence of any modern digital pathway lacks scientific justification. Justification for dedicated hardware remains valid for core accessories — hardware VU meters with XLR I/O, phantom-compatible passive faders, and portable earphone distribution amplifiers serve broadcast production requirements that software alone cannot replicate.

## Advice

TOMOCA Products occupies a narrow but legitimate professional niche: field broadcast accessories for the Japanese market with verified adoption by major broadcasters. The NPM-727 boundary microphone and TCC-100 passive audio fader represent competitive pricing in specialized categories where few alternatives meet broadcast-standard functional requirements.

Key limitations: the portfolio is entirely analog with no digital networking integration; most products do not publish THD, S/N ratio, or FR deviation data, making audio quality verification impossible from available information; the EM-9600 shotgun microphone's manufacturer-stated S/N ratio of 40 dB is inadequate for professional broadcast use; and support is limited to Japan with no documented international service infrastructure.

For Japanese broadcast production environments where hardware XLR compatibility, phantom power pass-through, and proven long-term professional reliability are the primary requirements, TOMOCA Products addresses a real operational need. Users requiring comprehensive measured performance data, modern digital connectivity standards, or international support should evaluate alternatives with more complete published specifications.
