## Overview

Heritage Audio is a professional studio equipment manufacturer founded in 2011 by Peter Rodriguez in Madrid, Spain. The company's stated mission is "To Make Vintage Sound possible for today's studios," centering its catalog on hardware recreations of 1960s–1970s British console circuitry — primarily Neve 1073-style preamps — alongside Fairchild 670-style tube compressors and Pultec-derived passive equalizers. Products are designed and hand-assembled at a dedicated facility near Madrid by a 15-person team, using Carnhill transformers and vintage-specification passive components. In 2022, Heritage Audio acquired the intellectual property of Lang Electronics, extending its vintage recreation scope into 1950s–60s American designs. A modern USB-C audio interface line (i73 PRO series) was introduced in 2024. The company distributes in 36+ countries through Sweetwater, Thomann, Vintage King, and KMR Audio.

## Scientific Validity

$$ \Large \text{0.5} $$

No independent third-party measurements are available for any Heritage Audio product from credible measurement sources. All figures below are manufacturer-published specifications only. Four representative products spanning different product categories and price tiers were evaluated.

**HA-73 Elite (mic preamp, 666 USD)** [2]: Manufacturer-specified frequency response of 20 Hz (+0.3 dB) to 20 kHz (−0.2 dB) represents excellent deviation across the full audio band. THD is specified at <0.025% at 1 kHz and <0.05% at 100 Hz — the 1 kHz figure is good, and the 100 Hz figure is acceptable. EIN is specified at <−125 dBu, which is good. Since these are manufacturer specifications without independent verification, evaluation is moderated accordingly.

**i73 PRO 2 (audio interface, 899 USD)** [3]: Dynamic range is specified at 116 dB (A-weighted) for mic and line inputs, and 125 dB (A-weighted) for the monitor output — both are excellent figures. Headphone output dynamic range is specified at >123 dB (A-weighted). EIN is −125 dBu. The manufacturer does not publish THD or frequency response specifications for this product, limiting the completeness of evaluation.

**Successor (stereo bus compressor, 1,499 USD)** [1]: Frequency response is specified at ±0.5 dB from 15 Hz to 30 kHz — a good result. THD+N is specified at <0.07% (50 Hz–20 kHz at +20 dBu output), the highest distortion figure among Heritage Audio's solid-state products evaluated here. Self-noise floor is specified at <−75 dBu, a limited figure for a professional bus compressor.

**HERCHILD 670 (stereo tube Vari-Mu compressor, 9,777 USD)** [1]: THD is specified at <1% at any level up to +18 dBu without limiting engaged. This level of harmonic distortion is clearly and unambiguously audible, representing a dominant characteristic of this unit's output rather than a negligible artifact. SNR is specified at 120 dB — an excellent result. Frequency response is ±1 dB from 20 Hz to 20 kHz, which is acceptable. The HERCHILD 670's THD specification is by design, reflecting intentional harmonic coloration from tube topology, but the objective measured performance remains the evaluation basis.

Across the four representative products, performance ranges from excellent (i73 PRO 2 dynamic range specifications) to clearly problematic (HERCHILD 670 THD <1%). The i73 PRO 2 and HA-73 Elite perform at good to excellent levels on their available specifications, while the Successor's THD+N and the HERCHILD 670's THD represent meaningfully elevated distortion. Averaging per-product assessments weighted by product importance — with the manufacturer-only data moderation applied throughout — yields an overall score of 0.5.

## Technology Level

$$ \Large \text{0.5} $$

Heritage Audio designs and assembles all products in-house at its Madrid facility, earning the in-house design credit. The company has accumulated 14 years of specialized expertise in vintage circuit recreation — including co-specifying bespoke Carnhill transformers and sourcing period-correct passive components — representing a genuine know-how base not easily replicated quickly. These positive factors are offset by two significant weaknesses. First, the core circuit topology of nearly every product is 50–70 years old: Neve 1073-style preamps, Fairchild 670-style vari-mu compression, Pultec passive EQ, and SSL-style diode-bridge compression are mature and widely replicated technologies, earning a deduction for technological recency. Second, claims that hand-wiring produces measurable sonic benefits — advanced without supporting measurement evidence — function as a marketing differentiator rather than a verifiable technical achievement, earning a further deduction for technology desirability. No proprietary patents have been identified. The i73 PRO family's USB-C and DSP integration uses commercially available components. No Heritage Audio technology is licensable or has been adopted by competitors. The positive adjustments (in-house design, know-how accumulation) cancel the negative adjustments (outdated foundations, marketing-only differentiation), leaving the score at the base of 0.5.

## Reliability & Support

$$ \Large \text{0.6} $$

Heritage Audio provides a standard 2-year manufacturer warranty covering defects in materials and workmanship, non-transferable, requiring prepaid return with proof of purchase. Thomann, a major global retailer, extends this to 3 years with in-house service for products purchased through their store.

Support operates primarily through an authorized distributor and dealer network — RAD Distribution for North America and Sea Distribution for Germany — rather than a broad direct global manufacturer service infrastructure. User forum documentation exists of extended non-response from Heritage Audio's direct support channels; positive resolution through regional distributors has also been documented. No statistical failure rate data is publicly available, and no product recalls have been issued in 14 years of operation.

Most Heritage Audio products use steel-chassis analog construction with discrete components and few moving parts, representing inherently simple and durable builds. Tube-based products (22-tube HERCHILD 670, TUBESESSOR, Grandchild 670) introduce inherent tube-aging degradation risk over time, and the mixed construction profile across the lineup nets a neutral adjustment. The i73 PRO family has received active firmware development, with a November 2024 update adding DSP plugin support, dual preamp operation, ADAT-to-S/PDIF conversion, and plugin delay compensation — demonstrating ongoing development for current digital products and earning a positive adjustment. The active firmware support is the primary adjustment above the base score.

## Rationality of Design Philosophy

$$ \Large \text{0.1} $$

Heritage Audio's stated mission — "Make Vintage Sound possible for today's studios" — is explicitly oriented toward reproducing the subjective character of 1950s–1970s recording equipment rather than advancing measurable audio fidelity. Five compounding factors reduce the score from the base of 0.5.

The primary technical approach centers on vacuum tubes, deliberately colored transformers, and vintage circuit topologies [1]. Contemporary Heritage Audio tube products including the HERCHILD 670N Next Gen (released December 2025) and TUBESESSOR continue to deploy vacuum tubes when solid-state alternatives at comparable or lower prices achieve substantially better measured distortion performance. Solid-state professional audio at high performance levels has been mature technology for decades, so no temporal context exception applies.

Cost allocation is directed toward achieving harmonic coloration rather than minimizing it. Carnhill transformers are co-specified explicitly for "correct harmonic distortion content and saturation points" — an objective to increase distortion in a specific character, not reduce it [1]. Custom vintage-specification polystyrene and polypropylene capacitors are sourced to reproduce vintage sound character, not to achieve lowest distortion. Hand-wiring in the 80 Series requires multi-day labor per unit, with no measurement evidence demonstrating audible benefit over PCB construction when electrical specifications are matched.

The design approach is maximally conservative. The entire core product line consists of recreations of equipment from 50–70 years ago. The 2022 Lang Electronics acquisition extended this philosophy further back into 1950s designs. The plugin expansion mirrors these same vintage circuit emulations in software rather than pursuing modern architectures or DSP-based innovations.

Multiple product claims assert audible benefit for elements that lack scientific verification [1]: "Class A sonic superiority" is not supported by objective measurement evidence when THD is within audibility thresholds; "vintage warmth" and "analog warmth" as desirable performance improvements lack ABX or blind test evidence; hand-wiring as an audible sonic differentiator has no measurement support.

The HERCHILD 670's 22-tube implementation and the 80 Series' multi-day hand-assembly process represent investment in quantity without demonstrated measurement performance advantage over modern solid-state alternatives at a fraction of the cost.

The i73 PRO family's integration of modern ADC/DAC and DSP into a vintage preamp chassis represents a genuine step toward functional modernization, but it does not substantially alter a company philosophy that is predominantly oriented toward coloration and vintage character as primary design goals over objective fidelity. Five negative adjustments from the base of 0.5 yield 0.0, which is clamped to 0.1 since Heritage Audio's products provide real, functional audio processing capability.

## Advice

Heritage Audio products are specifically designed for professional engineers who actively seek the harmonic coloration characteristics of 1960s–1970s recording console circuits as a creative tool. The HA-73 Elite and BritStrip serve this purpose for engineers who deliberately require the sonic character introduced by Carnhill transformer saturation. These products should be purchased with a clear understanding that they are intentional coloration devices, not transparent signal processors.

The i73 PRO 2 represents the strongest measured performance proposition in the Heritage Audio lineup. Its manufacturer-specified dynamic range of 116–125 dB (A-weighted) and 32-bit/192 kHz recording support are competitive specifications, and no direct equivalent at a lower price was confirmed. Engineers seeking a USB-C interface with integrated DSP and high dynamic range who are unconcerned with the 73-style preamp marketing designation may find the i73 PRO 2 worth evaluating.

Engineers seeking transparent, low-distortion signal chains for mastering, acoustic measurement, scientific audio work, or any application where minimum coloration is the goal will find Heritage Audio products unsuitable across most of the lineup. The HERCHILD 670 in particular should not be purchased for its measured audio performance — its manufacturer-specified THD is clearly audible by design — and its cost-performance ratio is extremely low relative to solid-state alternatives. The Warm Audio BUS-COMP achieves equivalent stereo bus compression functionality with substantially better measured distortion specifications at roughly one-twentieth the price. Similarly, the Focusrite ISA One provides equivalent preamp functionality to the HA-73 Elite with superior manufacturer-specified THD and EIN at 77% of the price, making it preferable for engineers who do not specifically require vintage transformer coloration.
